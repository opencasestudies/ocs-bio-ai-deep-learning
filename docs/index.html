Motivation

Aging is measurable long before it is visible. Although we often think of aging in terms of clinical symptoms, frailty, or disease risk, biological aging begins much earlier and unfolds across multiple molecular systems. One of the most reproducible molecular correlates of aging is DNA methylation, a regulatory chemical modification that alters gene activity without changing DNA sequence. Over the past decade, researchers have discovered that methylation patterns at specific regions in our DNA change systematically with age across populations, environments, and disease states.

These observations led to the development of epigenetic clocks: statistical models that estimate chronological age directly from DNA methylation profiles. The surprising accuracy of these clocks suggests that aging leaves a detectable imprint across the genome. Even more intriguingly, deviations between predicted age and chronological age may reflect biological differences in health status, environmental exposure, or disease processes.

This case study explores how machine learning models can learn those aging signatures from high-dimensional DNA methylation data. We begin with classical penalized regression models and then move toward deep neural networks capable of capturing nonlinear structure across thousands of DNA regions simultaneously.

DNA methylation-based epigenetic clocks have transformed aging research by providing quantitative markers of biological age that often outperform chronological age in predicting morbidity, frailty, and mortality. Although most established clocks to date rely on penalized linear regression (e.g., elastic net), recent works have demonstrated that deep learning may capture richer nonlinear relationships among DNA methylation sites, improving predictive accuracy.

This case study walks learners through reconstructing a neural network epigenetic clock model in the R statistical programming language. Learners will use scorcher, an R package designed for developing deep learning models with ease. The goal is to give the learners a hands-on experience working with DNA methylation data, introduce epigenetic clock models, teach modern neural network construction in R, and highlight practical modeling issues such as overfitting, interpretability, and biological validation.

Definition

Epigenetic clocks are statistical or machine learning models that estimate an individual’s biological age by learning systematic patterns in DNA methylation levels at specific genomic locations (CpG sites). These models leverage age-associated changes in methylation to produce an age estimate that can reflect not only chronological time but also aspects of biological aging, health status, and disease risk.

Main Question

This case study investigates whether DNA methylation profiles can be used to accurately predict chronological age across independent studies and populations using both classical and deep learning approaches.

Our main question(s)

  1. Can a neural network-based epigenetic clock accurately predict chronological age from publicly-available DNA methylation data?

  2. How does a deep learning-based epigenetic clock compare to a more traditional, elastic net-based clock when trained on the same, harmonized data?

  3. What practical challenges arise when applying deep learning model to high-dimensional biological data, and how can these challenges be addressed in a reproducible R workflow?

Context

What are Epigenetic Clocks?

DNA methylation is an epigenetic mechanism where methyl groups attach to DNA, affecting gene regulation without changing the DNA sequence. Across the genomes of many organisms, methylation levels change systematically with age. Early studies found that these changes were predictable enough to allow for the construction of statistical models that estimate age from methylation alone. These models became known as epigenetic clocks.

Epigenetic clocks are predictive models that estimate an organism’s biological age based on methylation patterns at specific regions in DNA (i.e., CpG sites, where a guanine nucleotide directly follows a cytosine nucleotide).

Early clocks focused on predicting chronological age, but over time, researchers have developed “next-generation” clocks that correlate not just with chronological age, but also with health outcomes, disease risk, and mortality. Today, epigenetic clocks play roles in:

  • Aging research, as biomarkers of biological aging
  • Exposure science, tracking environmental/behavioral influences
  • Clinical prediction, including mortality risk and disease progression
  • Comparative genomics, identifying conserved aging signatures across species

History of Epigenetic Clocks

In 2011, a study showed that DNA methylation in saliva could predict chronological age with an average error of ~5.2 years. Following this, in 2013, two landmark clocks were published: the blood-based Hannum clock and the cross-tissue Horvath clock. As the design of such clocks evolved, newer models now include biological signals coming from biomarkers, disease risk factors, and other lifestyle predictors to estimate biological age or age acceleration, which may better reflect functional aging, morbidity, and mortality.

Moving Beyond Classic Clock Models

Nearly all canonical clocks, including the Horvath, Hannum, PhenoAge, and GrimAge, are based on penalized linear regression models. As there are often more CpG sites (~850k) than available samples (###), we cannot directly fit a traditional linear regression model. Penalized models, such as the elastic net model, identify subsets of sites (~100-500) with methylation values that are predictive of chronological age. Penalized linear regression models such as the elastic net have several advantages. Namely, they:

  • Have good performance in high-dimensional settings,
  • Perform automatic feature selection,
  • Are interpretable,
  • Are computationally inexpensive,
  • Generalize reasonably across studies and tissues.

However, the biology of epigenetics is complex, and methylation patterns arise from regulatory networks, chromatin structures, and biological interactions that are often nonlinear processes. Linear models ignore interactions between CpG sites, as the genome is organized as a set of physically interacting DNA strands in three-dimensional space. Further, they struggle with multicollinearity among related features, often leading penalization methods to arbitrarily select one CpG from a group of similarly behaving sites. As research advances, more flexible modeling approaches may better capture the complexity of methylation dynamics. Recent works have begun moving beyond linear models to deep learning, as deep learning may capture patterns that linear models might miss. Specifically, deep learning models can:

  • Model nonlinear relationships,
  • Capture interactions between CpG sites,
  • Incorporate multi-omic features,
  • Leverage architectures reflecting genomic structure.

Epigenetic clocks are among the most successful predictive models in modern molecular epidemiology. Since the introduction of early clocks such as the Horvath multi-tissue clock and the Hannum blood-based clock, researchers have increasingly used methylation-based age prediction to study disease risk, environmental exposure, and biological resilience.

The central insight behind epigenetic clocks is that aging is not random at the molecular level. Instead, methylation changes follow reproducible trajectories across populations. Some CpG sites exhibit monotonic increases in methylation with age, while others decrease predictably. When aggregated across thousands of genomic locations, these changes form a stable signal that can be learned statistically.

Early epigenetic clocks relied primarily on penalized regression models. Elastic net regression proved particularly effective because it handles correlated predictors well and performs automatic feature selection. However, methylation signals arise from regulatory networks rather than independent CpG effects. This motivates the use of neural networks capable of representing interactions among genomic loci.

DeepMAge represents one of the earliest demonstrations that deep neural networks can outperform classical clocks in predicting age from blood methylation profiles. It trained a multilayer perceptron on nearly five thousand samples drawn from seventeen GEO studies and evaluated performance on more than one thousand independent samples from fifteen additional studies. The reported median absolute error of approximately three years highlights the remarkable predictive power of methylation-based models.

This case study reconstructs the conceptual workflow behind that analysis using publicly available GEO datasets and modern deep learning infrastructure in R.

From Statistical Modeling to Machine Learning

At a high level, machine learning is the practice of using data to learn patterns that can be used to make predictions, classify observations, or discover structure. In this case study, we focus on one form of machine learning, supervised learning, where each observation comes with both input features (predictors) and a known outcome. Here, our input features are DNA methylation values at CpG sites, and our outcome is chronological age.

A supervised learning problem can be written conceptually as:

Or, in our case:

The epigenetic clock model learns a function that maps a methylation profile to a predicted age. During training, the model sees many examples where the true age is known. It adjusts its internal parameters so that its predictions become closer to the observed ages.

This setup is very similar to familiar statistical modeling approaches. For example, in linear regression, we estimate coefficients that relate predictors to an outcome. Machine learning uses the same general idea but often emphasizes prediction, generalization, and performance on new data. Rather than asking whether a particular CpG site is statistically associated with age, we ask whether the model can accurately predict age for samples it has never seen before.

This distinction is especially important in biomedical data science. A model that fits our training data well may still fail on a new cohort if it has learned study-specific artifacts, batch effects, or technical noise. For this reason, we evaluate models using held-out test studies, not only the same samples used during training.

In this case study, the learning task is concrete:

Given a person’s blood DNA methylation profile, can we predict their chronological age?

This makes epigenetic clock construction an excellent machine learning example, as the outcome is interpretable, the data are biologically meaningful, and model errors are measured in years.

What Makes Omics Data Hard for Machine Learning?

DNA methylation data are high-dimensional. A single sample may contain tens or hundreds of thousands of CpG measurements. In contrast, the number of individuals is usually much smaller. This produces a common problem in genomics in that we have many more features than samples. Here, for example, we have 4,930 training samples and 24,538 CpG sites, so the model has access to far more predictors than observations. This is very different from many classical regression settings, where the number of observations is much larger than the number of covariates.

High-dimensional omics data create several challenges:

  1. First, many CpG sites are correlated with each other. Nearby CpGs may share
    regulatory context, and distant CpGs may be coordinated through cell-type
    composition, immune activity, disease state, or developmental programs. A
    model must therefore handle strong dependence among predictors.
  2. Second, not all CpG sites are informative for age. Some are highly
    predictive, some are weakly predictive, and many may contribute mostly noise.
    Without feature selection or regularization, a model may overfit by assigning
    importance to random variation.
  3. Third, technical variation can be substantial. Methylation arrays are
    affected by platform differences, laboratory processing, normalization
    choices, and batch effects. A model trained across multiple studies must
    learn age-related methylation signals while avoiding study-specific artifacts.
  4. Fourth, biological interpretation is subtle. A CpG site that improves age
    prediction is not necessarily causal for aging. It may be a marker of cell
    composition, immune shifts, environmental exposure, developmental regulation,
    or correlated molecular change.

These challenges motivate the modeling choices in this case study. Elastic net regression gives us a strong, interpretable baseline for high-dimensional data. Deep learning gives us a more flexible nonlinear model. Cross-study validation helps test whether either model generalizes beyond the studies used to train our epigentic clock.

What are the data?

All data for this case study come from public DNA methylation datasets hosted on the National Center for Biotechnology Information’s (NCBI) Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) repository, located at www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/. GEO is a public archive of functional genomics experiments.

Specifically, we have curated data from 32 studies which contain 6,411 DNA methylation profiles in total. Among these, we designated 17 studies, consisting of 4,930 samples, for use as our training set. We designated the other 15 studies, consisting of 1,293 profiles, as our testing set. In both the training and testing sets, we will focus only on those samples annotated as being part of the control cohorts for their original studies. Further, we will focus only on data obtained from blood samples on Infinium Human Methylation 450K and 27K BeadChip (Illumina) platforms and only those studies with available age metadata.

Each study contains DNA methylation measurements generated using Illumina HumanMethylation450 or EPIC BeadChip arrays.

These arrays measure methylation levels at hundreds of thousands of CpG sites across the genome. At each CpG site, the assay produces a beta value between zero and one representing the fraction of methylated DNA molecules observed at that locus.

Each sample therefore consists of:

a vector of CpG beta values a reported chronological age study-level metadata describing sample collection and processing

Across all thirty-two studies included in the DeepMAge-style workflow, the combined dataset contains thousands of individuals spanning a wide age range. Importantly, these studies were conducted independently, often using different laboratory conditions and preprocessing pipelines. This creates a realistic setting for evaluating whether predictive models generalize beyond their training environment.

In the full workflow:

seventeen studies form the training set fifteen studies form the testing set

Limitations

Although methylation-based age prediction is highly accurate, several limitations affect interpretation. As such, there are some important considerations regarding this data analysis to keep in mind:

  1. First, methylation patterns differ across tissues. Most public methylation
    datasets are derived from whole blood. Clocks trained on blood samples may
    not generalize to brain, liver, or muscle without modification.
  2. Second, technical variation across laboratories introduces batch effects
    that may obscure biological signal. Even after normalization, residual
    differences between studies can influence prediction accuracy.
  3. Third, chronological age is not equivalent to biological age. Prediction
    error reflects both model uncertainty and genuine biological heterogeneity.
  4. Fourth, deep learning models require careful validation to avoid
    overfitting. High-dimensional predictors increase flexibility but also
    increase the risk that models capture study-specific noise rather than shared
    aging signatures.

Recognizing these limitations is essential for interpreting the results of this study, and more broadly, epigenetic clocks, within the appropriate context.

Ethical Considerations

Epigenetic clocks illustrate both the promise and the complexity of applying machine learning to human molecular data. As such, there are some important ethical considerations when working with data relating to this case study’s main questions.

  1. Because methylation patterns encode information about aging, disease
    exposure, and environmental history, predictions derived from these data may
    reveal sensitive biological characteristics. Even anonymized genomic data
    remain potentially identifiable and therefore require careful stewardship.
  2. Another ethical concern involves representativeness. Many public
    methylation datasets disproportionately include individuals of European
    ancestry. Models trained on such datasets may perform differently across
    populations, raising concerns about fairness and generalizability.
  3. There is also a conceptual distinction between prediction and explanation.
    A model that predicts age accurately does not necessarily explain the
    biological mechanisms of aging. Communicating this distinction clearly is
    essential when presenting results to clinical or public audiences.

Responsible use of machine learning in genomics therefore requires transparency, reproducibility, and careful interpretation.

Data Analysis

In this section, we move from data preparation to predictive modeling. Our goal is to learn how patterns in DNA methylation across thousands of CpG sites can be translated into accurate estimates of chronological age. Because methylation data are high-dimensional and biologically structured, no single modeling strategy is sufficient on its own. Instead, we hope to build an understanding progressively by starting with a strong classical baseline model and then introducing more flexible nonlinear approaches. We begin with elastic net regression, which provides an interpretable and historically important foundation for epigenetic clock construction. We then introduce neural networks, which extend linear models by learning nonlinear combinations of CpG features. This leads naturally into deep learning, where layered architectures allow models to capture increasingly complex structure in genomic data. Finally, we discuss overfitting and regularization, two central concepts that determine whether predictive models generalize beyond the datasets on which they were trained. Throughout, we hope to provide both the statistical intuition and computational tools needed to understand how modern epigenetic clocks are constructed.

Elastic Net Regression

Before introducing neural networks, we begin with elastic net regression, as it is central to the history of epigenetic clocks and remains one of the most important tools for high-dimensional biological prediction.

Elastic net is a type of regularized linear regression model. In traditional linear regression, we model an outcome as a weighted sum of predictors:

\[ \text{Age} = \text{Intercept} + \theta_1 \times \text{CpG}_1 + \theta_2 \times \text{CpG}_2 + \ldots + \theta_p \times \text{CpG}_p \]

Each CpG receives a coefficient. Positive coefficients indicate that higher methylation at that CpG site is associated with older age, while negative coefficients indicate that higher methylation is associated with younger age.

The difficulty is that ordinary regression breaks down when there are more predictors than samples, or when predictors are highly correlated. DNA methylation data have both problems. Elastic net addresses this by adding a penalty that discourages the model from becoming too complex.

Elastic net combines two penalties:

  • L1 Penalty: Encourages sparsity
  • L2 Penalty: Encourages shrinkage and stability

The L1 penalty, also used in lasso regression, can set some coefficients exactly to zero. This performs feature selection by removing CpG sites that do not contribute strongly to prediction.

The L2 penalty, also used in ridge regression, shrinks coefficients toward zero without necessarily eliminating them. This is helpful when predictors are correlated, because it stabilizes coefficient estimates and prevents the model from relying too heavily on any single CpG site.

Elastic net is useful because it balances both behaviors. It can select a subset of predictive CpG sites while also handling correlation among methylation sites.

In the context of epigenetic clocks, elastic net has several advantages. It is computationally efficient, relatively interpretable, and well suited to settings where the number of CpG sites is much larger than the number of samples. As we saw, many foundational epigenetic clocks used elastic net-style models for exactly these reasons.

However, elastic net is still a linear model. It assumes that CpG contributions are added together in a simple weighted sum. That assumption may be too restrictive for biological systems where regulatory mechanisms interact across genomic regions, cell states, and developmental pathways.

This limitation motivates our next class of models, neural networks.

Neural Networks

A neural network is a flexible predictive model built from layers of simple mathematical transformations. The term neural network comes from an analogy to the brain: just as biological neurons receive signals from other neurons, combine those signals, and pass messages forward through interconnected circuits, artificial neural networks pass information through layers of computational ‘neurons’ that transform inputs step by step. Although neural networks are often described using this brain metaphor, they are best understood as adaptable function approximators, that is, mathematical systems that learn how to transform input data, such as CpG methylation measurements, into useful predictions like chronological age.

In this case study, the input to the neural network is a vector of DNA methylation measurements collected across many CpG sites, and the output is a single predicted value representing chronological age. Rather than modeling age as a simple weighted sum of these measurements, a neural network learns a sequence of transformations that gradually reshape the information in the data. Each layer combines signals from many CpG sites, adjusts their importance through learned weights, and applies a nonlinear transformation that allows the model to capture complex biological relationships. By stacking multiple layers together, the network can represent patterns that are far more flexible than those available to standard linear regression models, making it especially well suited for high-dimensional genomic data.

Conceptually:

A hidden layer learns an intermediate representation of the data. Instead of directly predicting age from CpG sites, the model first transforms the DNA methylation values into new features. These learned features may capture combinations of CpG sites, nonlinear trends, or coordinated methylation patterns.

When we stack multiple hidden layers, we create a deep neural network. Each layer transforms the representation from the previous layer. Early layers may learn simpler combinations of CpGs, while later layers may learn more abstract patterns useful for age prediction.

For DNA methylation data, this is appealing because aging is unlikely to be encoded by independent CpG effects alone. Methylation patterns are shaped by chromatin state, cell composition, gene regulation, immune aging, developmental programs, and environmental exposures. A neural network can, in principle, learn nonlinear relationships among these signals.

However, flexibility comes with tradeoffs. Neural networks can overfit, require more computation, and are harder to interpret than elastic net models. They also require more modeling decisions: number of layers, number of neurons, activation functions, learning rate, dropout, batch size, optimizer, and regularization.

Deep Learning: Why “Deep” Matters

The word deep in “deep” learning refers to the use of multiple layers. A “shallow” model may transform the input once before prediction. A deep model transforms the input repeatedly, allowing each layer to build on the previous one.

In image recognition, early layers might learn edges, intermediate layers might learn shapes, and later layers might learn object parts. In methylation data, the analogy is less visual but still useful. Early layers may learn combinations of nearby or correlated CpG sites. Later layers may learn broader patterns related to immune composition, developmental regulation, or age-associated methylation remodeling.

A deep learning model is trained by minimizing a loss function. In this case study, the loss is based on absolute prediction error:

\[ \text{Absolute Error} = |\text{True Age} - \text{Predicted Age}| \]

The model adjusts its weights to reduce this error across the training samples. This adjustment is done using an optimization algorithm such as Adam.

The training process proceeds iteratively. The model makes predictions, calculates errors, updates weights, and repeats. Over many epochs, the model gradually improves its predictions.

Important training concepts include:

Epoch: One full pass through the training data.

Batch Size: The number of samples used for each weight update.

Learning Rate: How large each update step should be.

Optimizer: The algorithm used to update model weights.

Loss Function: The quantity the model tries to minimize.

Validation Set: Held-out data used to monitor performance during training.

Test Set: Independent data used for final evaluation.

These concepts are not specific to our motivating methylation data, but central to modern deep learning across many different applications. A goal of this case study is to make these ideas concrete by applying them to a biologically meaningful prediction task.

Overfitting and Regularization

One of the most important ideas in machine learning is overfitting.

A model overfits when it learns patterns that are specific to the training data but do not generalize to new data. In genomics, overfitting is especially easy because there are many features and often relatively few samples. A model can accidentally learn technical artifacts, study-specific batch effects, or random noise.

Overfitting can look impressive during training. The model may have very low error on the training set. But when applied to new studies, performance may collapse.

Regularization refers to methods that reduce overfitting by limiting model complexity.

Elastic net uses regularization directly through coefficient penalties. Neural networks use several forms of regularization, including dropout, weight decay, early stopping, and validation monitoring.

Regularization strategies for neural netwroks:

Dropout randomly removes a fraction of hidden units during training. This prevents the network from relying too heavily on any one pathway through the model. It encourages the network to learn more robust representations.

Weight decay penalizes large weights, encouraging smoother and less extreme models.

Cross-validation repeatedly splits training data into fitting and validation subsets, helping evaluate whether model performance is stable.

Why Compare Elastic Net and Deep Learning?

A central pedagogical theme of this case study is comparison. Deep learning should not be introduced as a replacement for statistical modeling. Instead, it should be introduced as one part of a modeling toolkit. To understand what deep learning contributes, we need meaningful comparisons.

We compare to elastic net for several reasons:

  1. First, it is historically central to epigenetic clocks. Many widely used
    clocks were developed using penalized regression.
  2. Second, it performs well in high-dimensional settings. A neural network
    that only slightly outperforms a simpler model is not very informative.
  3. Third, elastic net is easier to interpret. Its coefficients directly
    indicate which CpG sites contribute to the prediction and in what direction.
    Neural networks are more flexible but less transparent.
  4. Fourth, elastic net is computationally efficient. If elastic net performs
    similarly to deep learning, then the added complexity of neural networks may
    not be justified for some applications.

This comparison encourages critical thinking. We do not assume that deep learning is universally better. Instead, we ask when additional flexibility improves prediction enough to justify increased complexity.

Introducing Scorcher

Deep learning is often taught using Python frameworks such as TensorFlow, Keras, or PyTorch. These tools are powerful, but many biomedical researchers, statisticians, and data scientists work primarily in R. Switching programming languages can create additional barrier and steeper learning curves.

The scorcher package is designed to make deep learning more accessible in R by building on the torch ecosystem while providing a higher-level modeling interface. Scorcher aims to make neural networks easier to define, inspect, train, modify, compare, reuse, and teach. It emphasizes model construction through ‘tidy’ composable functions. This allows users to focus on the structure of the model: what the inputs are, what layers are added, what activation functions are used, what loss is optimized, and how predictions are evaluated.

In this case study, scorcher serves two roles: 1. First, it is the computational tool used to build the neural network
epigenetic clock. 2. Second, it is a teaching framework that makes the deep learning architecture
visible. Each line in a scorcher pipeline corresponds to a modeling decision.

For example, a model pipeline might say:

model |>
  scorch_layer(linear, in_features = 1000, out_features = 512) |>
  scorch_layer(relu)                                           |>
  scorch_dropout(0.3)                                          |>
  scorch_layer(linear, in_features = 512,  out_features = 512) |>
  scorch_layer(relu)                                           |>
  scorch_dropout(0.3)                                          |>
  scorch_layer(linear, in_featues = 512, out_features = 1)

Here, we can see that the model takes 1,000 CpG sites as input, transforms them into a 512-dimensional hidden representation, applies a nonlinear (relu) activation, uses dropout for regularization, and ultimately returns one predicted age.

A Note on Tidy Function Composition and Piping in R

Many R users are familiar with tidy workflows where data transformations are written as a sequence of readable steps. For example:

data |>
  filter(!is.na(age)) |>
  mutate(age_years = as.numeric(age)) |>
  select(sample_id, age_years)

This is convenient because it makes analysis pipelines easier to read. Each line describes one transformation. The output of one step becomes the input to the next. Scorcher extends this design philosophy to neural networks. Instead of treating model architecture as a hidden object created all at once, we build it step by step. This mirrors how we think conceptually:

Start with inputs |>
  Add a dense layer |>
  Add a nonlinear activation |>
  Add regularization |>
  ... Repeat ...
  Add output layer |>
  Compile |>
  Train |>
  Evaluate |>

Piping also encourages modularity. If we want to change the model, we can add, remove, or modify one step. For example, we can change dropout from 0.3 to 0.5, replace the type of activation function, or add another hidden layer. This makes experimentation natural, but it also improves reproducibility: a model architecture written as a pipeline can be read, reviewed, and discussed.
